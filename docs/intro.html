1.1 Summary

This week introduces basic concepts about remote sensing, physical principles, resolution of remote sensing data and data selection constraints, and familiarity with the software we will use.

1.1.1 Remote sensing

Remote sensing is the acquiring of information from a distance. NASA observes Earth and other planetary bodies via remote sensors on satellites and aircraft that detect and record reflected or emitted energy. Remote sensors, which provide a global perspective and a wealth of data about Earth systems, enable data-informed decision making based on the current and future state of our planet.
(Source: NASA. What is Remote Sensing? | Earthdata (nasa.gov))


(Source: ESA. Copernicus Missions: Sentinel-1 images global landmasses, coastal zones and shipping routes with a C-band sensor)

Advantage of Remote Sensing

  • Data
    • Large area and time coverage.
    • Frequency of updates.
    • Easy data acquisition.
    • Provide useful information in various wavebands.
    • Repetitive coverage provides to monitor dynamic themes like agriculture.Stereo satellite data may be used for three-dimensional studies.
  • Data process
    • Free processing resources like GEE.
  • Policy
    • Policy decesions being made using Remote Sensing.

Disadvantage of Remote Sensing

  • Expensive for small areas.
  • Requires specialized training for analysis of images.
  • Aerial photos are costly if repetitive photos are required.

1.1.2 Overall view of working remote sensing system

  1. The origin of electromagnetic energy (sun transmitter carried by the sensor).
  2. Transmission of energy from the atmosphere of the earth.
  3. Interaction of energy with the earth‚Äôs surface (reflectance/absorption/transmission) or self-emission.
  4. Transmission of the reflectance/emitted energy to the remote sensor.
  5. Detection of energy by the sensor, converting it into a photograph may be digital or analog.
  6. Transmission/recording of the sensor output.
  7. Pre-processing of the data and generation of the data product.
  8. A collection of ground truth and other collateral information.
  9. Data analysis and interpretation.
  10. Various themes, or other applications.

1.1.3 Sensor

Sensors, or instruments, aboard satellites and aircraft use the Sun as a source of illumination or provide their own source of illumination, measuring energy that is reflected back. Sensors that use natural energy from the Sun are called passive sensors; those that provide their own source of energy are called active sensors.


(Source: NASA. What is Remote Sensing? | Earthdata (nasa.gov))

Push Broom and Whisk Broom Sensors (originally taught in Chapter 3, I‚Äôve moved it here for ease of memory)

Whisk broom scanners, also sometimes referred to as spotlight or across track scanners, use a mirror to reflect light onto a single detector. The mirror moves back and forth, to collect measurements from one pixel in the image at a time.

Push broom scanners, also sometimes referred to as along track scanners, use a line of detectors arranged perpendicular to the flight direction of the spacecraft. As the spacecraft flies forward, the image is collected one line at a time, with all of the pixels in a line being measured simultaneously. One drawback of push broom sensors is that the detectors in the ‚Äúpush broom‚Äù can have varying sensitivity. If they are not perfectly calibrated, this can result in stripes in the data.


(Source: RF Wireless World)

1.1.4 Electromagnetic

Electromagnetic energy, produced by the vibration of charged particles, travels in the form of waves through the atmosphere and the vacuum of space. These waves have different wavelengths (the distance from wave crest to wave crest) and frequencies.


(Source: NASA. What is Remote Sensing? | Earthdata (nasa.gov))

  • EMR Interacting with Earth‚Äôs surface The EMR, on interaction with earth‚Äôs surface, experiences a number of changes in the magnitude, direction, wavelength, polarization and phase etc.
    • Absorption
    • Transmission
  • EMR Interacting with Atmospheric Before radiation used for remote sensing reaches the Earth‚Äôs surface it has to travel through some distance of the Earth‚Äôs atmosphere. Particles and gases in the atmosphere can affect the incoming light and radiation. These effects are caused by the mechanisms of scattering and absorption.
    • Scattering
      • Rayleigh scattering
      • Mie scattering
      • Nonselective scattering
    • Absorption
      • Ozone

1.3 Reflection

This week‚Äôs teaching was interesting and fundamental, showing us the potential and applications of remote sensing technology. In China, as the spatial planning reform of the country progresses, planning authorities are placing increasing emphasis on remote sensing and 3D GIS technology.

For many years there has been a contradiction between land use planning and urban planning in China. With increased urbanisation, in particular, the conflict between arable land and urban built-up areas. In recent years, Chinese officials have undertaken a reform of the planning system, combining the two into one, called territorial spatial planning. Remote sensing has changed the way of territorial spatial planning, and its powerful data acquisition, analysis, prediction and management capabilities provide data, methods and management platforms to support territorial spatial planning.Remote sensing helps to achieve a refined and intelligent management of the whole cycle of territorial spatial planning.

Planning policies have been integrated to form a ‚Äúone map‚Äù for territorial spatial planning based on one map, which can be opened up in layers.
